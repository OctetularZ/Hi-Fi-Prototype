Voyage of John Cabot

1497

Sailing from Bristol aboard the Matthew in 1497, Italian explorer John Cabot (Giovanni Caboto) embarked on a voyage that led to the discovery of parts of North America—likely Newfoundland. Sponsored by King Henry VII, his journey marked one of the earliest European expeditions to the continent since the Norse. Cabot’s voyage cemented Bristol’s role in maritime exploration and opened the door to England’s future claims in the New World. Today, a replica of the Matthew is docked in Bristol Harbour as a tribute to this historic journey.

Bristol Riots

1831

Sparked by the rejection of the second Reform Bill, the Bristol Riots were among the most violent protests in 19th-century England. Thousands gathered to oppose the political elite, leading to the burning of major buildings and widespread looting. The chaos lasted three days and resulted in over 100 arrests and several executions. The riots highlighted the city’s demand for political reform and helped pressure Parliament to pass the Reform Act of 1832, changing the way cities like Bristol were represented.

Bristol Blitz

1940-41

During World War II, Bristol was heavily bombed in a series of German air raids known as the Bristol Blitz. Its strategic port and aircraft factories made it a prime target. The attacks destroyed over 80,000 buildings, including homes, churches, and the city’s iconic Dutch House. More than 1,300 people lost their lives. Despite the devastation, the community showed remarkable resilience. The Blitz permanently changed the city’s landscape and left a legacy of wartime architecture, shelters, and stories of courage.

Clifton Suspension Bridge

1864

The Clifton Suspension Bridge was completed in 1864, five years after the death of its visionary designer, Isambard Kingdom Brunel. Spanning the dramatic Avon Gorge, the bridge is a masterpiece of Victorian engineering and quickly became an icon of Bristol. Originally conceived to connect Clifton with Leigh Woods, it now symbolizes the city’s innovation and ambition. With its stunning views and architectural beauty, it attracts visitors from around the world and remains one of the most photographed landmarks in the UK.

Temple Meads Station

1840

Opened in 1840, Bristol Temple Meads was designed by Isambard Kingdom Brunel as the western terminus of the Great Western Railway. The station transformed travel between London and the West Country, making Bristol more accessible and boosting trade and industry. As one of the oldest surviving railway stations in the world, it reflects the grandeur and innovation of the early rail era. Today, it remains a major transport hub and a testament to Bristol’s central role in Britain’s industrial history.

Completion of SS Great Britain

1843

The SS Great Britain launched from Bristol in 1843 and revolutionized sea travel. Designed by Brunel, it was the first large ship built of iron and powered by a screw propeller. At the time, it was the largest vessel in the world. The ship sailed across the Atlantic and later carried emigrants to Australia. After decades of abandonment in the Falklands, it returned to Bristol in 1970. Now fully restored, it is a popular museum and a symbol of the city’s maritime legacy.
